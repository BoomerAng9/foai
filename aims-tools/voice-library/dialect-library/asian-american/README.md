# Asian American — Group root

> Asian American Anglophone register across origins. Korean, Vietnamese,
> Chinese (Mandarin / Cantonese / Hokkien / Hakka), Japanese, Filipino,
> South Asian (Indian / Pakistani / Bangladeshi / Sri Lankan / Nepali),
> Southeast Asian (Thai / Khmer / Lao / Indonesian / Malaysian / Burmese),
> Hmong, Mongolian. Heritage-language influence + generation effects +
> regional US concentration shape register.

## Scope

In scope:
- **East Asian American** — Korean / Japanese / Chinese (Mandarin,
  Cantonese, Hokkien, Hakka, Shanghainese).
- **Southeast Asian American** — Vietnamese, Thai, Khmer, Lao,
  Indonesian, Malaysian, Burmese, Filipino, Hmong.
- **South Asian American (Desi)** — Indian (multiple states / language
  substrates: Hindi / Punjabi / Gujarati / Tamil / Telugu / Bengali /
  Marathi), Pakistani (Urdu / Punjabi / Sindhi), Bangladeshi (Bengali),
  Sri Lankan (Sinhala / Tamil), Nepali.
- **Pacific Islander American** — Hawaiian, Samoan, Tongan, Filipino-
  Pacific, Chamorro / Guamanian. (Pacific Islander often grouped with
  Asian American in US census but linguistically distinct; tracked
  here for now, may split into own group later.)
- **Diaspora generation register** — first-gen immigrant, 1.5-gen
  (immigrated as kids), second-gen US-born, third-gen+.
- **Regional concentration**: SF Bay / LA / NYC / Houston / Atlanta /
  Seattle Asian-American registers.

Out of scope:
- Pure heritage language (no English) — separate
  internationalization.
- MENA / Persian / Turkish (lives under `mena/`).
- Central Asian (Kazakh / Uzbek / etc.) — future expansion.

## Register / intensity scale

Asian American register sits on a **substrate-influence scale 0-3**
plus a **generation axis** (1st / 1.5 / 2nd / 3rd+):

| Level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | Standard professional | Asian-Am speaker, no detectable substrate. Common in 2nd-gen+ professional contexts. |
| **1** | Light substrate | Detectable accent + occasional vocabulary loans (*ah-yo*, *aiyaa*, *yaar*, *bhai*). Casual professional. |
| **2** | Conversational substrate | Substrate prosody clearly audible, frequent code-switch with heritage language, sentence patterns from heritage audible. |
| **3** | Heavy heritage / first-gen | Strong heritage prosody dominant; first-generation immigrant register; may code-switch into heritage language. |

| Generation | Typical effect |
|---|---|
| **1st-gen** | Heritage-substrate register typical at level 2-3; may speak with heavy accent. Pre-immigration register intact. |
| **1.5-gen** | Immigrated as child; bilingual fluent; register code-switches widely. |
| **2nd-gen** | US-born to immigrant parents; register often level 0-1 in English (sometimes can't speak heritage language). May "code-switch up" to heritage register at home. |
| **3rd-gen+** | Often level 0; heritage-language fluency rare; register often blends with regional US Anglo-American register. |

## Phonetic / cadence signatures (group-level)

(To be expanded per regional sub-file. Major patterns:)

- **Tone-language substrates** (Mandarin, Cantonese, Vietnamese, Thai,
  Hmong) audible as distinctive sentence-melody in first-gen English.
- **/r/-realization** — varies (Japanese / Korean often softer, Indian
  often retroflex, Filipino often Spanish-influenced).
- **Vowel positions** — Indian English has distinctive /e/ and /o/
  vowels; Korean-Am 1st-gen often has /æ/ shift.
- **Consonant clusters** — first-gen often simplified.
- **Discourse markers** — *aiyaa* (Mandarin / Cantonese disappointment),
  *yaar* (Hindi friend), *lah* (Chinese-Singapore-Malaysia), *ah-yo*
  (Korean), *po*/*opo* (Filipino respect markers).
- **Indian English-specific cadence** — tracked under
  `regional/south-asian-am-desi.md` per the specific register.

## Gatekeeping notes

- **National-origin specificity** — calling a Korean-American character
  "Asian" generic is a brand miss. Specific country / heritage matters.
- **Generation specificity** — 1st-gen / 1.5 / 2nd / 3rd+ register is
  vastly different. Don't assume.
- **East Asian / SE Asian / South Asian** are NOT interchangeable.
  Vietnamese is not Chinese; Indian is not Filipino; Korean is not
  Japanese.
- **Filipino register specificity** — Filipino-American has long US
  colonial history (1898-1946), distinctive register that overlaps
  with Spanish AND English systems. Treat distinctly from East Asian.
- **South Asian sub-region** — Punjabi / Gujarati / Tamil / Bengali /
  Malayalee Indian-Am have distinct heritage-language substrates.
  Pakistani-Am Punjabi differs from Indian-Am Punjabi mostly by
  religious-cultural overlay, not language base.
- **Pacific Islander identity** — politically distinct from Asian
  American (US Census groups them but communities push for AAPI as
  the umbrella; Pacific Islanders maintain distinct ethnic identity).

## Anti-patterns

- Don't render Asian American characters with "model minority" or
  "math nerd" tropes. Brand-damaging and stereotype-reinforcing.
- Don't write all East Asian as monolithic — Korean / Chinese /
  Japanese register is distinctly different.
- Don't write Indian English with stage *Apu*-style mockery. The
  actual register is rich and present-day.
- Don't deploy *aiyaa* / *yaar* / *lah* on non-heritage characters
  for flavor.
- Don't conflate Filipino with East Asian. Filipino-Am has its own
  register lineage.
- Don't write Asian-Am 2nd-gen with heavy 1st-gen accent — they
  often speak General American with no detectable substrate.

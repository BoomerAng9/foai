# URBANISM — Southern, Atlanta

> Atlanta is the gravity well of contemporary Black American culture.
> Hip-hop, college, finance, broadcast media, megachurch all coexist
> within ~30 miles. The dialect range here is wider than any single
> Atlanta voice — what binds them is the cadence: deliberate, steady
> rhythm, slight pull on stressed syllables, comfortable with silence.
> Atlanta speech is *measured* in a way New York or LA Black speech
> often isn't.

## Sub-registers within Atlanta

### 1. Atlanta professional / "the firm"
- **AAVE intensity**: 1-2 primary, 0 in client meetings, 3 with family.
- **Cadence**: deliberate, slightly slower than New York. Comfortable
  with two-second pauses.
- **Phonetic markers**: monophthongization of *-ay* (*ride* → *raahd*;
  *time* → *taahm*). *-ing* often → *-in*. Final consonants soften
  but don't drop entirely.
- **Vocabulary markers**: *the firm*, *take a meeting*, *tap in* (NEW),
  *for sho* (occasional), *I hear you*, *say less*.
- **Anchors for ear-training**: Killer Mike off the album; Stacey
  Abrams at podium; Andrew Young historical recordings.

### 2. Atlanta hip-hop / trap-adjacent
- **AAVE intensity**: 3-4. Heavily code-marked; AAVE is the operating
  language, not the second one.
- **Cadence**: slower than East Coast hip-hop, faster than Houston.
  Triplet flow influence. Pausing ON a beat instead of between beats.
- **Phonetic markers**: aggressive monophthongization (*right* →
  *raht*, *fine* → *fahn*). *-er* endings soft (*brother* → *brutha*
  but with full second syllable). Heavy nasalization.
- **Vocabulary markers**: *bag* (money), *cap* / *no cap*, *trap*
  (anywhere you grind), *plug*, *the wave*, *foreal foreal*, *period*.
- **Anchors**: T.I. interviews; Killer Mike musical-vs-spoken;
  Big Boi (Outkast) measured tempo; Jeezy long-form
  conversational.

### 3. Atlanta megachurch / preacher cadence
- **AAVE intensity**: 2-4 with controlled rise. Builds from low to
  high; preacher cadence pulls deep AAVE features out for emphasis.
- **Cadence**: long phrases ending in stressed monosyllables. The
  "and-the-Lord-said *PEACE*" beat. Comfortable with congregational
  call-and-response timing.
- **Phonetic markers**: extended vowels on stress points
  (*pee-eace*, *bro-thuh*). Final-consonant emphasis when the line
  is rhetorically complete (*"and that's *what*"*).
- **Vocabulary markers**: *brother / sister*, *the Lord said*,
  *come on now*, *ain't that right*, *somebody say amen*, *touch
  your neighbor*, *you can have a seat*. Theological lexicon (*the
  blood*, *the Word*, *anointing*, *seasons*).
- **Anchors**: Bishop T.D. Jakes; pastor Andy Stanley (compare —
  white Atlantan, no AAVE, related cadence); Bishop Eddie Long
  (historical); Charles Stanley (historical, white but Atlanta cadence).

### 4. Atlanta street / young / SWATS
- **AAVE intensity**: 4-5. Dense; AAVE is the operating system.
- **Cadence**: faster than Atlanta professional, with AAVE-prosodic
  end-of-clause pitch drops. Comfortable with ellipsis (drops what
  doesn't need to be said).
- **Phonetic markers**: full monophthongization, *-er* deletion
  (*brother* → *brutha*), *th-* fronting (*think* → *fink*; not
  universal — generation-marked). Vowel laxing in stressed
  syllables.
- **Vocabulary markers**: *foreal* (intensifier), *bruh*, *chill*,
  *that ain't it*, *period*, *deadass* (newer, NE-influenced),
  *finna* (heavy), *we outside*.
- **Anchors**: Lil Baby interviews; Gucci Mane recent
  conversational (post-prison cleaner register);
  21 Savage in long-form sit-down podcasts;
  barbershop YouTube content.

## Cross-cuts

- **Generation matters.** Older Atlantans (60+) carry deeper Southern
  AAVE features — *bin*-perfective, *ax* (ask), *th-* fronting,
  monophthongization more uniform. Younger Atlantans (under 30)
  blend trap-region prosody with national AAVE features and Internet
  loanwords.
- **Class doesn't fully predict register.** A street-raised lawyer
  and a megachurch pastor's daughter can both code-switch through
  most levels. The default operating point reflects who they're
  *currently with*, not just who they grew up as.
- **Atlanta speech is recognizable on ANYONE.** The slow-drag
  monophthongs, the comfortable silence, the *-in'* endings — these
  travel across class, education, gender, age.

## When to assign this dialect

- Any Coastal or FOAI character set in/near Atlanta.
- Megachurch / faith-leader register (cross-reference
  `URBANISM/subcultural/church.md`).
- Hip-hop or trap-music adjacent characters.
- Black Atlanta professional in a code-switched-to-formal moment.
- NOT Coastal Brewing's primary cast (Coastal lives in Bluffton/
  Beaufort/Savannah, with Coastal Georgia pulling some Savannah
  Black-Lowcountry register — see `southern-coastal-georgia.md`).

## Anti-patterns

- Don't import LA or NYC AAVE features (e.g. NY *deadass*, *bodega*,
  rapid clip; LA *cuz*, *bruh-bruh*, valley-Black blend) into an
  Atlanta voice. Atlanta is its own thing.
- Don't write Atlanta speech as monolithic. The professor and the
  rapper share a city, not a register.
- Don't use *finna* in level 1-2 contexts. *Finna* is a level 3+
  marker; in a professional voice it reads as performance.

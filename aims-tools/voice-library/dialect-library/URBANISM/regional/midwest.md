# URBANISM — Midwest (Detroit + Chicago + St. Louis)

> Midwest Black-American hip-hop register is two anchor cities and a
> handful of secondary scenes. **Detroit** and **Chicago** are the
> heavy-lexicon poles; St. Louis sits adjacent in vowel system to
> Memphis (cross-Mississippi-River cousin register). Detroit and
> Chicago each carry multiple sub-registers — Detroit splits Eastside
> vs Westside, and Chicago splits drill vs non-drill.
>
> Common ground: Great Migration cadence (Mississippi → Chicago, then
> Detroit / cross-Great-Lakes spread) sits underneath everything.
> Both cities carry Black-Pulpit cadence as a load-bearing layer
> (Civil Rights Black political oratory has Midwest origins via Jesse
> Jackson, Louis Farrakhan, Harold Washington).

---

## Sub-region 1: Detroit (the D / the 313 / Motown City)

### Cadence + acoustic profile
- **Pacing**: middle — faster than Houston, slower than NYC.
- **Pitch**: adult-male F0 typically **95-145 Hz**.
- **Phonetic markers**:
  - **Detroit raising / Northern Cities Vowel Shift** influence —
    *cot* and *caught* distinct (unlike LA's merger), *bag* with
    raised vowel (*beg*-adjacent).
  - **R-coloring strong** (Midwest pattern, distinct from
    Down-South *r*-dropping).
  - *-ing* → *-een* in casual speech (similar to Bay Area but
    less consistent).

### Detroit hip-hop register

**Origin**: Detroit's hip-hop scene built outward from late-1980s
underground roots. The mid-1990s **J Dilla** (James Yancey, deceased
2006) and **Slum Village** (T3, Baatin, Jay Dee/Dilla) defined a
Detroit underground sound that combined warped soul / jazz samples
with off-beat drum programming — the foundation later picked up by
Pete Rock, Q-Tip, D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, Pharrell.

In parallel, the late-1990s **Bad Meets Evil** duo (Royce da 5'9"
+ Eminem) put Detroit on the lyrical-rap map. **Eminem** (Marshall
Mathers, 8 Mile / Westside Detroit) extended it nationally. Modern
Detroit hip-hop runs heavy on Eastside / Westside neighborhood
identity and a distinct musical style (Detroit-style production
is its own thing — sample-flip-heavy, fast tempos, distinct from
trap and drill).

**Detroit greeting / register signature**:
- *"What up doe?"* — Detroit's traditional greeting. *"Doe"* /
  *"dough"* as term of address (*"What up doe, brother?"*).
  This is to Detroit what *"What's good?"* is to NYC. Strong
  identity marker.

**Vocabulary markers**:

| Term | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| *what up doe* / *what up tho* | Detroit greeting | THE Detroit-marked phrase. *Doe* / *tho* used as term of address. |
| *doe* / *tho* | term of address | Detroit-specific (parallels NYC *son* / Bay *pimpin'*). |
| *the D* / *Detroit* / *the 313* | self-reference | *"The D"* most casual; *"the 313"* hip-hop-marked. |
| *Eastside* / *Westside* | neighborhood-identity poles | Detroit Black hip-hop is split along these lines; ongoing labels-and-affiliations beef. *"Eastside Chedda Boyz"* vs *"Street Lord'z"* is canonical. |
| *Motown* | Detroit + the soul music heritage | Self-reference with cultural-pride weight. |
| *the Town* | also Detroit | Less common than *the D*; some confusion with Oakland's *the Town*. |
| *cuz* | term of address | Same as LA usage; less gang-marked in Detroit than in LA. |
| *Chedda* / *Chedda Boyz* | money / Eastside Detroit collective | Affiliation marker for Eastside lineage. |
| *Street Lord'z* | Westside Detroit collective | Affiliation marker. |
| *bro* | term of address | Detroit uses *bro* heavily in casual register. |
| *that bag* | money / income / opportunity | Modern Detroit; spread genre-wide. |
| *gettin' to the bag* | making money | Detroit-strong. |
| *finna* | "fixing to" | Shared with broader AAVE; Detroit uses heavily. |
| *the Lou* | St. Louis (cross-reference) | Adjacent register. |

**Cadence markers**:
- **Detroit lyricism is hyper-verbal** — Eminem-style fast, dense,
  rhyme-stacked delivery is a Detroit signature. Royce da 5'9" carries
  it conversationally as well.
- **Storytelling vs Street** — Royce-frame article terminology:
  Detroit lyricism splits into hyper-lyrical storytelling
  (Eminem / Royce / Big Sean's narrative work) and direct street
  (Street Lord'z / Eastside Chedda Boyz lineage). A Detroit-rooted
  character should sit at one or the other, not blend mid-turn.
- **Code-switch range often wide** — Detroit professional Black
  speech runs from AAVE 0 to 4 fluidly.

**Operating-register modulation**:
- **Customer-facing** — *what up doe* greeting OK customer-facing
  for warmth (it's recognizable as Detroit-marker but
  non-opaque); other neighborhood-affiliation vocabulary OFF.
- **Detroit-rooted character internal** — full home register at 3-4.
- **Bag vocabulary** (*the bag*, *gettin' to the bag*) is now
  genre-wide and OK at most surfaces; *Chedda Boyz* / *Street
  Lord'z* affiliations off customer-facing.

**Gatekeeping**:
- *Chedda Boyz* / *Eastside Chedda Boyz* — direct Eastside
  Detroit collective affiliation; gatekeeping-tight (per the
  ongoing lineage dispute, getting it wrong is socially expensive).
- *Street Lord'z* — direct Westside Detroit collective.
- *What up doe* is widely deployable but only by Detroit-rooted
  characters; outside-Detroit deploy reads as cosplay.

**Anchors for ear-training**:
- Royce da 5'9" — Drink Champs full episode (his Allegory press
  tour episode is canonical; long discussion of Detroit's beef
  scene).
- Eminem — long-form interviews are rare but exist (Sway in the
  Morning, Joe Rogan podcast, Stephen A. Smith). His conversational
  register (vs. battle-rap register) is the cleaner reference.
- Big Sean — Detroit Eastside-rooted; multiple long-form podcasts.
- Danny Brown — Westside Detroit; The Danny Brown Show podcast
  he hosts is the cleanest Detroit-conversational register
  available.
- Slum Village (T3 surviving) — interview circuit on Dilla legacy.
- Tee Grizzley — modern Detroit; Drink Champs.
- Kash Doll — modern Detroit female register.

## Sub-region 2: Chicago (Chi / the Chi / the 312)

Chicago Black-American register has TWO heavy lexically-distinct
sub-registers, plus a Black-political / Civil-Rights-elder register
heritage:

1. **Chicago non-drill / "old" Chicago** — Common, Lupe Fiasco,
   Twista, Kanye West (Kanye is Chicago-rooted in conversational
   register), Vic Mensa.
2. **Chicago drill** — Chief Keef, Lil Durk, G Herbo, Lil Reese,
   Fredo Santana, King Louie, FBG Duck.

These two have related underlying register but diverge in lexicon,
tempo, gang-affiliation density, and gatekeeping rules. They are
documented separately below.

### Cadence + acoustic profile (Chicago general)
- **Pacing**: mid-tempo on conversational, drill-sub-register goes
  fast and clipped.
- **Pitch**: adult-male F0 **95-150 Hz**.
- **Phonetic markers**:
  - **Northern Cities Vowel Shift** (shared with Detroit) —
    *bag* / *beg* / *bog* vowels distinct.
  - *-ing* → *-in'* / *-en* in casual register.
  - **Chicago R-coloring** — strong word-final *r*.

### Chicago non-drill register

**Vocabulary markers**:

| Term | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| *the Chi* / *Chi-town* | Chicago | Self-reference. |
| *the 312* | Chicago (older area code) | Hip-hop-marked. |
| *the South Side* / *the West Side* | Chicago neighborhood-identity poles | Heavy in Black Chicago register. |
| *jeet-yet?* | "Did you eat yet?" (multi-ethnic Chicago phrasing) | Working-class Chicago marker, used by Black Chicagoans casually. |
| *the L* | the Chicago elevated train | Geographic marker. |
| *fa real* / *for real for real* | intensifier | Common Chicago register. |
| *bro* / *bruh* | term of address | Standard. |
| *cake* / *gettin' that paper* | money | Standard. |
| *coast* (verb) | move easily / glide | Chicago-marked usage. |

**Anchors for non-drill Chicago register**:
- Common — multiple long-form Black Thought interviews, Drink
  Champs, NPR Tiny Desk, motivational keynote circuit.
- Kanye West — earlier conversational interviews (College Dropout
  press tour) are cleanest Chicago register.
- Lupe Fiasco — Drink Champs, multiple academic-leaning
  interviews; he speaks at AAVE 1-2 with strong Chicago-vowel
  signature.
- Twista — Drink Champs (he's the speed-rap originator;
  conversational register is much slower).
- Chance the Rapper — long-form interviews; modern Chicago
  professional register at AAVE 1-2.
- Vic Mensa — interviews on activism and politics.

### Chicago drill register — the gatekept sub-register

**Origin**: emerged 2011-2012 from Chicago South Side. **Chief
Keef** (Keith Cozart) released *I Don't Like* (2012) which broke
the drill scene mainstream. Other foundational figures: **Lil
Durk** (Lil Durk's foundational era includes *Signed To The
Streets* 2013 mixtape), **G Herbo**, **Lil Reese**, **Fredo
Santana**, **King Louie**, **FBG Duck** (deceased).

Producer **Young Chop** defined the early sound: booming 808 kicks,
minor-key melodies, tempos around 60-70 BPM. Lyrics: direct,
unfiltered narrative of street violence, *Chiraq* (Chicago + Iraq)
neighborhood reality.

**Etymology of "drill"**: in Chicago gang culture, **drill** =
killing another gang member, taken from former Chicago gangster
**Al Capone**'s vocabulary. The term predated drill music in local
slang; the music genre took the name.

**Vocabulary markers**:

| Term | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| *drill* / *drillin'* | to kill (gang context) / by extension the music | Capone-era origin; modern drill scene reanchored it. |
| *opp* / *opps* | opposition / enemy gang member | Drill-coined (Keef / Durk / Herbo 2011-2013); now spread genre-wide and beyond hip-hop. |
| *smoke* / *smokin' [opps]* | to kill an opp | Chief Keef-popularized; now genre-wide. |
| *Chiraq* | Chicago + Iraq (war-zone metaphor) | Chicago South Side self-reference; politically controversial (not all Chicagoans accept the framing). |
| *4 / four* | denial / opposition (No. *4* ≠ allies) | Chicago drill numerology. |
| *6 / six* (number) | shorthand for various gang lineages depending on speaker | Number-affiliation marker. |
| *no cap* | no lie | Genre-wide now but Chicago drill heavy use. |
| *bag* | money / kilogram of contraband | Drill-marked; spread. |
| *gang gang* | affirmation / agreement / loyalty | Drill-coined exclamation. |
| *on God* / *on gang* | sworn intensifier | Drill-marked. |
| *the bricks* | the streets / the neighborhood | Chicago-marked. |
| *the sticks* | guns | Drill-coined. |
| *backend* | money owed / proceeds | Drill register. |
| *reach* | attempt revenge / hit a target | Drill register. |
| *the moves* | criminal activity | Drill register. |

**Gatekeeping** (register-authenticity, same logic as Thun /
Wu / Dipset gatekeeping):
- **Direct affiliation references** (specific set names,
  numbered sets, named-individual references) are direct-
  affiliation markers. Only deploy on a character with
  documented connection — same logic as Mobb Deep affiliation
  (Thun) or Wu-Tang Clan affiliation. Mismatched deploy reads
  as cosplay.
- **Generic drill vocabulary** (*opp*, *smoke*, *no cap*, *gang
  gang*, *the bag*) has spread across hip-hop. Using these on a
  non-drill character is a register-mismatch but not gatekept.

**Operating-register modulation**:
- **Customer-facing** — drill register off (audience comprehension
  + register mismatch). *Chiraq* off in any contested-framing
  context.
- **Hip-hop cultural surface** — diffused drill vocabulary OK
  for a drill-rooted character.
- **Internal team** — depends on character context.

**Anchors for ear-training**:
- Chief Keef — interviews (sparse; he's notoriously interview-
  reluctant). Wack 100 Clubhouse, ALL OUT Show.
- Lil Durk — Drink Champs, Joe Budden, R&B Money. Modern
  conversational register sometimes professional (post-musical-
  success code-switch).
- G Herbo — Million Dollaz Worth of Game, Drink Champs.
- King Louie — interview circuit on drill origin.
- FBG Duck (deceased) — heritage drill register; archival
  recordings for ear-training only, not current.
- Vic Mensa — speaks ABOUT the drill scene (he's Chicago but
  not drill-affiliated); useful for context-setting register.

## Sub-region 3: St. Louis (the Lou / the 314)

### Brief
- **Pacing**: similar to Memphis (both on the Mississippi River
  corridor — register-cousin).
- **Phonetic signature**: **the *r*-substitution** — *here* →
  *hurr*, *there* → *thurr*, *care* → *currr*. Nelly's foundational
  *Country Grammar* aesthetic.
- **Anchors**: Nelly (early 2000s), Chingy, Murphy Lee, modern
  scene less hip-hop-prominent.
- **Vocabulary markers**: *the Lou*, *the Stl*, *errr* (vowel
  filler), *St. Louis Tennessee* (cross-Mississippi-River register
  blend pattern with Memphis).

This file does not go to depth on St. Louis — register is small in
hip-hop terms and largely cousin-shares with Memphis.

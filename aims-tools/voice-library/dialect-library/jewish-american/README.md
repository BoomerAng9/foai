# Jewish American — Group root

> Jewish American Anglophone register across heritage lineage and
> regional concentration. Ashkenazi (NYC / LA / Boston / Chicago /
> Florida), Sephardic (NYC / LA / Atlanta), Mizrahi (LA / NYC),
> Modern Orthodox, Hasidic / Haredi (Brooklyn Crown Heights /
> Borough Park / Williamsburg / Monsey / Lakewood / Israel), Israeli-
> American (cross-cuts MENA), Soviet-Jewish-American (NYC Brighton
> Beach / LA West Hollywood / Chicago).

## Scope

In scope:
- **Ashkenazi heritage** — Yiddish-substrate English; NYC heavy
  concentration; LA / Chicago / Boston / Florida secondary.
- **Sephardic heritage** — Ladino-substrate; smaller US population;
  NYC / LA / Atlanta concentration.
- **Mizrahi heritage** — North African / Middle Eastern Jewish; cross-
  cuts with `mena/`; smaller US population.
- **Modern Orthodox** — observant but engaged with broader American
  culture; distinct register from secular Jewish-Am.
- **Hasidic / Haredi** — Yiddish often as primary language;
  Williamsburg Satmar, Crown Heights Chabad, Borough Park Bobov,
  Lakewood Lithuanian-yeshivish; distinct register from secular and
  Modern Orthodox.
- **Yeshivish** — Modern Orthodox / yeshiva-student English with
  heavy Hebrew + Aramaic + Yiddish vocabulary intermixed.
- **Israeli-American** — Israeli first-gen + 1.5-gen US, Hebrew-
  substrate (cross-references `mena/regional/israeli.md` for the
  Israeli side; Jewish-American identity layer is here).
- **Soviet-Jewish-Am** — Russian-substrate Jewish-American; arrived
  1970s-2000s; Brighton Beach (NYC), West Hollywood (LA), Chicago
  Skokie / Buffalo Grove.

Out of scope:
- Pure Israeli (not US-resident) — lives under `mena/regional/israeli.md`
- Pure Yiddish (no English) — separate internationalization.
- Russian Jewish in Russia (not US) — separate.

## Register / intensity scale

Jewish American register sits on a **substrate-influence scale 0-3**
plus a **religiosity dimension**:

| Substrate level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | Standard professional | Jewish-Am speaker, no detectable Yiddish/Hebrew substrate. Common in 3rd-gen+ professional contexts. |
| **1** | Light heritage | Detectable rhythm + occasional vocabulary loans (*oy*, *schlep*, *kvetch*, *mensch*, *chutzpah*, *schmooze*). NYC Ashkenazi default. |
| **2** | Conversational heritage | Substrate prosody clearly audible, frequent vocabulary code-switch (*nu?*, *shvitz*, *shtetl*, *farklemt*), discourse markers. Casual conversation. |
| **3** | Heavy heritage / first-gen | Strong heritage prosody dominant (Yiddish-substrate or Hebrew-substrate or Russian-substrate); first-generation immigrant register or Hasidic community register. |

| Religiosity dimension | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Secular** | Identity-cultural Jewish; vocabulary may surface in cultural-context but no religious-register density. |
| **Reform / Conservative** | Some religious-register vocabulary (synagogue context, holidays); register-aware. |
| **Modern Orthodox** | More religious-register vocabulary in casual speech; Hebrew terms (*tefilah*, *kashrut*, *halacha*). |
| **Hasidic / Haredi** | Yiddish as primary or co-equal language; register-dense; English may carry strong Yiddish substrate. |

## Phonetic / cadence signatures (group-level)

(To be expanded per regional sub-file. Major patterns:)

- **Yiddish-substrate prosody** — sentence-final rising-then-falling
  intonation, distinctive cadence patterns, "Yinglish" word
  formations (*schmaltzy*, *schlocky*).
- **Question-as-statement** — Yiddish-substrate Anglophone often
  forms questions through intonation contour rather than syntactic
  inversion (*"You should worry?"* meaning *"Should you be
  worried?"*).
- **Discourse markers** — *nu?* (so?), *oy* / *oy vey* (oh dear),
  *feh* (disgust), *meh* (whatever — Yiddish-Am origin, now
  mainstream), *bisl* (a little).
- **Hebrew-substrate prosody (Israeli-Am)** — different from
  Yiddish-substrate; Modern Hebrew has its own rhythm.
- **Russian-substrate (Soviet-Jewish-Am)** — different again from
  Yiddish; consonant cluster handling, /v/-/w/ realization.
- **Hasidic / Haredi register** — Yiddish words integrated freely
  with English, distinct discourse patterns (*should you live and
  be well*), repetition-as-emphasis.

## Gatekeeping notes

- **Religious-register specificity** — Hebrew prayer references
  (*Shema*, *kaddish*, *Birkat Hamazon*) are sacred; deploying them
  on casual / non-religious characters reads as cosplay or
  blasphemy.
- **Hasidic-specific register** — Haredi communities have their own
  internal speech patterns that don't translate cleanly to broader
  Jewish-Am register; rendering them outside that community context
  is fraught.
- **Yiddishisms vs heritage substrate** — Yiddishisms (schlep, oy,
  kvetch, mensch, chutzpah) have spread genre-wide and are mainstream
  English; that's NOT the same as actual heritage-substrate prosody.
- **Anti-Semitism trope-avoidance** — careful with money-related
  vocabulary, schmoozing-as-manipulation framings, etc. Standard
  trope-avoidance applies.
- **Israeli vs American Jewish** — politically distinct identities;
  Israeli-Am first-gen has Hebrew-substrate; American Jewish
  doesn't. Don't conflate.
- **Sephardic vs Ashkenazi** — vastly different heritage; *Most
  American Jews are Ashkenazi (~90%)* but Sephardic identity is
  distinct and present.

## Anti-patterns

- Don't render Jewish characters with anti-Semitic tropes (greedy,
  scheming, money-obsessed, world-controlling). Brand-damaging.
- Don't write all Jewish characters as NYC comedy register. Religious
  / Hasidic / Sephardic / Israeli-Am are vastly different.
- Don't use *Jewish princess* / *JAP* (acronym) or other gendered
  stereotypes. Stereotype-reinforcing and offensive.
- Don't deploy Hebrew prayer language as flavor.
- Don't write Hasidic characters as monolithic / quaint. Modern
  Hasidic communities are diverse and present-day.
- Don't conflate Jewish identity with Israeli identity. Many American
  Jews have no Israeli connection; many Israeli citizens are not
  Jewish.

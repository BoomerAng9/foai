# African (continental) — Group root

> African continental Anglophone register: Nigerian, Ghanaian, Kenyan,
> South African, Ethiopian-Eritrean (Amharic / Tigrinya substrate),
> Senegalese (French-substrate), Cameroonian (bilingual EN-FR),
> Ugandan, Tanzanian, Zimbabwean, Liberian. NOT a single register —
> Africa has 1.5+ billion people across 54 countries with massive
> linguistic diversity. This group covers Anglophone-Africa speakers
> + Anglophone-diaspora speakers in the US/UK/Canada.

## Scope

In scope:
- Nigerian English + Nigerian Pidgin (Naija) — Lagos / Yoruba / Igbo /
  Hausa register variants.
- Ghanaian English — Akan substrate, Twi-influenced cadence.
- Kenyan English — Sheng (Swahili-English code-switch register), urban
  Nairobi vs rural.
- South African English — distinct from Afrikaans-English; Cape Town vs
  Joburg; Black SA vs White SA registers.
- Ethiopian-Eritrean — Amharic / Tigrinya substrate.
- Senegalese / Ivorian — French-substrate Anglophone speakers.
- Cameroonian — bilingual EN-FR contact register.
- East African — Tanzanian, Ugandan, Rwandan (where English is widely
  spoken).
- Zimbabwean — distinctive register, Shona / Ndebele substrate.
- Liberian — historic Americo-Liberian English influence.
- US-African diaspora register: Nigerian-American, Ghanaian-American,
  Ethiopian-American (heavy DC concentration), Somali-American
  (Minneapolis).

Out of scope:
- Pure French / Portuguese / Arabic Africa (separate
  internationalization tracks).
- North African (Maghreb) — lives under `mena/`.

## Register / intensity scale

African continental register sits on a **substrate-influence scale 0-3**:

| Level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | Standard British/American English | African speaker, no detectable substrate. International / diplomatic / academic register. |
| **1** | Light substrate | African accent + occasional vocabulary loans, but English is the operating system. *I dey go*, *you go like am*, *abi?* in Nigerian; *eish*, *howzit* in SA. |
| **2** | Conversational substrate | Substrate prosody clearly audible, vocabulary code-switch frequent, sentence patterns from heritage language audible. |
| **3** | Heavy substrate / Pidgin | Full Naija Pidgin, full Sheng, etc. Native-context register; may be hard to parse for non-substrate speakers. |

Plus a **diaspora-blend dimension**: US-born or US-raised Africans
often blend home register with URBANISM markers (e.g., Nigerian-
American second-gen with NYC-specific code-switching).

## Phonetic / cadence signatures (group-level)

(To be expanded per regional sub-file. Major patterns:)

- **Tone-language substrate** in Nigerian Yoruba/Igbo, Ghanaian Twi,
  Kenyan Bantu — surface in English as distinctive sentence-melody.
- **Vowel positions** more peripheral than General American (less
  reduction in unstressed syllables).
- **/r/-realization** varies — Nigerian rolling, Ghanaian alveolar,
  South African non-rhotic in some registers.
- **Yes/no question** intonation — flat or falling in some African
  Englishes (vs General American rising).
- **Discourse markers** — *abeg* (Nigerian), *eish* (South African),
  *kwani?* (Kenyan).

## Gatekeeping notes

- **Country specificity** — calling a Nigerian character "African"
  generic is a brand miss. Specific country / ethnic group matters.
- **Pidgin / Sheng deploy** — comprehension wall on customer
  surfaces unless audience is heritage. Modulate per
  `REGISTER-MODULATION.md`.
- **Diaspora generation** — first-gen vs second-gen vs third-gen
  changes register significantly. Don't assume.
- **Tribe / ethnic-group specificity** within country — Yoruba vs
  Igbo vs Hausa Nigerian register matters; Kikuyu vs Luo vs Kalenjin
  Kenyan register matters. These are register markers, not just
  identity labels.
- **Coloured (term)** in South African context is a documented
  ethnic / racial group naming under apartheid-era classification —
  it's used in-context but is sensitive in US discourse.
  Render with cultural awareness.

## Anti-patterns

- Don't render all Africans in Nigerian register — Nigeria is one of
  54 countries.
- Don't write Trevor Noah's distinctive Joburg Coloured register as
  generic "African accent." His register is documented and specific.
- Don't deploy Naija Pidgin or Sheng on customer-facing surfaces
  without comprehension-aware modulation.
- Don't write African characters with generic "wise elder" tropes.
  Render real present-day register.
- Don't use *Yahoo Yahoo* / *419* / *Nigerian prince* tropes.
  Brand-damaging and stereotype-reinforcing.
